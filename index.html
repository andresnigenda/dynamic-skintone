Moving up the ladder? How skin tone determines social mobility in Mexico

In Mexico, there is persistent social stratification by skin color. Lightest-skinned Mexicans have, on average, 1.5 additional years of schooling and earn 53% more per hour than their darkest-skinned peers. It is no surprise then, that having a darker skin tone is related to lower upward mobility in Mexico. However, these inequities are accentuated for some and attenuated for others according to their sociodemographic characteristics.

This project explores the different dimensions and breakdowns of social mobility for Mexicans aged 25 to 64 in 2016 using data from Mexico's National Institute of Statistics and Geography (INEGI). The 2016 Module on Intergenerational Social Mobility (MMSI) lets us map the perceived socioeconomic status of individuals at age 14 and compare it to their current perceived status. The MMSI also provides information on the perceived skin color using the PERLA scale, which assigns skin color according to a palette with eleven skin tones.

Each bucket on the matrix is colored according to the average skin color of the population it represents. For example, in 2016, 0.11% of Mexicans perceived themselves to be at the highest socioeconomic status when they were at the lowest at age 14, and the average skin tone of this subset of the population was 7.79, which corresponds to color number 8 on the PERLA scale.

The lighter your skin tone, the more likely you are to be better off, or at least not worse off than when you were 14 y.o.

If we look at all Mexicans aged 25 to 64, we can notice two things. First, the bucket with highest economic mobility has a lighter average skin tone than the bucket with the highest downward mobility. Second, buckets in the upper part of the matrix (buckets with higher upward mobility and lower downward mobility) have a skin tone that is, on average, lighter than the ones on the bottom half.

Social mobility for darker-skinned women is lower than for their darker-skinned peers

For women, the overwhelming majority of higher upward mobility and lower downward mobility buckets are lighter skinned on average. That is, if you are a Mexican woman that was born with a darker skin tone, not only are you more likely to not go up the ladder of social mobility when compared to your lighter-skinned peers, but you are also more likely to have a lower socioeconomic level, and stay there.

Afromexicans have an even more reduced spectrum of upward mobility

According to the latest inter-census survey, 1.2% of Mexico's population identifies as afro-descendants. Given the previous patterns that we have observed, it is not surprising to see that even among Afromexicans, lighter-skinned Afromexicans have better mobility outcomes than darker-skinned ones. Even worse, there are gaps in the higher upward mobility and lower income mobility buckets.

Mexicans with a high socioeconomic status are lighter-skinned

In addition to perceived socioeconomic status, the MMSI survey classifies dwellings into four strata according to specific socioeconomic characteristics of the people living in them as well as the physical characteristics and equipment of the dwellings based on 24 variables recovered from the census.

Mexicans at the highest socioeconomic stratum are whiter than the average Mexican. Furthermore, the bin with the highest mobility, where individuals went from having the lowest perceived socioeconomic level at age 14 to the highest current level had an average skin tone of 9.39 out of 11, where 11 is the lightest possible skin tone.
